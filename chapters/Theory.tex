\section{Quantum Chromodynamics}

  Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD) is the theory that describes the strong interaction, the force that binds quarks and gluons into hadrons. As is often done to understand more complicated theories, we draw parallels and analogues to the simpler and more familiar theory of Quantum Electrodynamics (QED). Just as QED describes the interaction between electrically charged particles with the photon acting as the mediator, QCD describes the interaction between colour charged particles (quarks and gluons) with the gluon acting as the mediator. The underlying symmetry group of QED is U(1), which gives rise to a single degree of freedom (electric charge) and one generator that refers to a single massless gauge boson (the photon). However, when studying the $\Delta^{++}$ baryon consisting of three up quarks ($uuu$) with aligned spins, it is clear that an additional quantum number is needed to satisfy the Pauli exclusion principle. On top of this, to ensure that the total wavefunction is antisymmetric as baryons are fermions, this new quantum number must have at least three different ``charges''. Compared to QED, where we denote the singular electric charge with simply +/-, in QCD we havecolour charge, with the fundamental charges being labeled as red, green, and blue. These are often shortened to simply $r$, $g$, $b$, and the respective anti-colours $\overline{r}$, $\overline{g}$, $\overline{b}$. 

  The underlying symmetry group of QCD is SU(3), where the 3 refers to the three colour charges. Compared to U(1), SU(3) has 8 generators, leading to 8 massless gauge bosons known as gluons. Unlike photons in QED, gluons themselves carry colour charge, which means they may interact with each other.   \subsection{Asymptotic freedom}
    Arguably one of the biggest misnomers in particle physics is the term ``coupling constant''. This quantity describes the strength of an interaction, and as the name suggests it was originally thought to be a constant value. However, it is now well established that the coupling ``constant'' actually varies with the energy scale of the interaction, a phenomenon known as the running of the coupling constant. This running of the coupling is present even in QED, where the coupling slightly increases with the energy scale of the interaction. This effect can be calculated by considering higher order corrections to the basic interaction vertex, specifically by including fermion loops in the propagator, see Fig. \ref{fig:qedloops}. These fermion loops lead to a screening of the electric charge, as virtual electron-positron pairs align themselves along the electric field, effectively reducing the observed charge at larger distances (corresponding to lower energy scales).

    to consider when building these fermion loops: the different photon loops cancel out, so we only need to consider the fermion loops in the propagator. In QCD however, the presence of triple and quadruple gluon vertices means that we also need to consider gluon loops in the propagator, see Fig. \ref{fig:qcdloops}. It turns out that these gluon loops have the opposite effect of fermion loops: they lead to an anti-screening of the colour charge, increasing the effective coupling at larger distances (lower energy scales). The strong coupling as a function of the energy scale $q^2$ is given by 
    \begin{align}
      \alpha_s(q^2) = \frac{\alpha_s(\mu^2)}{1 + \beta_0\alpha_s(\mu^2)\ln\left(\dfrac{q^2}{\mu^2}\right)}, \label{eq:runcoupling}
    \end{align}
    where $\mu^2$ is the reference energy scale and $\beta_0$ is defined as
    \begin{align}
      \beta_0 = \frac{11N_c - 2N_f}{12\pi},  \label{eq:beta0}
    \end{align}
    with $N_c$ the number of colours (3 in QCD), and $N_f$ the number of quark flavours. Equation \ref{eq:runcoupling} can be simplified by introducing the QCD scale parameter $\Lambda_\text{QCD}$, which is defined as the energy scale where the denominator of Eq. \ref{eq:runcoupling} vanishes. Experimentally, the value of $\Lambda_{QCD}$ is approximately 200-300 MeV. With this definition, we can rewrite Eq. \ref{eq:runcoupling} as
    \begin{align}
      \alpha_s(q^2) = \frac{1}{\beta_0\ln\left(\dfrac{q^2}{\Lambda_\text{QCD}^2}\right)}. \label{eq:runcouplingsimplified}
    \end{align}
    From this equation it is clear that as $q^2$ increases, the logarithm in the denominator also increases, leading to a decrease in the strong coupling $\alpha_S$. Another way to phrase this is to say that at high energy scales colour charges become asymptotically free, as the coupling becomes very weak. Figure \ref{fig:alphas} shows the running of $\alpha_S$ along with multiple determinations at various energy scales. In the regime where $\alpha_S \ll 1$ we can use perturbative QCD (pQCD) to make calculations, as higher order corrections are suppressed by powers of the strong coupling. At low energy scales however, $\alpha_S$ becomes large and perturbative QCD fails to converge. This regime is often referred to as non-perturbative QCD or soft QCD. As first principles calculations are impossible in this regime, we often resort to phenomenological models combined with MC event simulations to make predictions that can be compared to experimental data. 

    In the context of high energy particle physics experiments at the LHC, perturbative QCD is applicable in the initial hard scattering between 2 partons from the colliding protons and most of the subsequent parton showering, while the hadronisation consists of mainly non-perturbative processes.

  \subsection{Colour confinement and the QCD potential}

    The fundamental particles that carry colour charge, quarks, are never observed in isolation. This phenomenon is known as (colour) confinement, which states that only colour-singlet states may exist as free particles. Colour singlet states are combinations of (anti)colours that are invariant under rotations in colour space. In other words, applying any transformation from the SU(3) group should leave the state unchanged. 
    % \pc{Note the key concept here is that they are invariant under rotations in color space and they, therefore, need to be color neutral}
    In the case of mesons consisting of a quark-antiquark pair, the colour singlet state is given by $\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}(r\overline{r} + g\overline{g} + b\overline{b})$. This is the \textit{only} possible way to combine a quark and an antiquark into a colour singlet, as invariance under rotations in colour space is a more stringent requirement than simply requiring the state to be colour neutral. Imagine a transformation that simply exchanges two colours, for example $r \leftrightarrow g$. This would transform a state given by $r\overline{r}$ into $g\overline{g}$. Naively one could say that both states are colour neutral, so no harm no foul, but clearly the state is not invariant under this transformation, and hence not a colour singlet. Similarly for baryons, which consist of three quarks, there is only one colour singlet state given by $\frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}(rgb - rbg + gbr - grb + brg - bgr)$ \mvl{Not sure how useful it is to write this out; this is shorthand for a much more elaborate equation; maybe just state that there is also exactly one singlet state for 3-quark baryon states}.\rs{Indeed it's not really useful, but when I would read that there is exactly one solution I would be curious to see what it looks like.     There is no first principles argument for colour confinement. It stands to reason that this originates from the gluon self-interactions. To motivate this we turn our attention to the non-relativistic QCD potential between two colour charges, which can be expressed as a sum of two terms:
    \begin{align}
      V(r) = \pm C \frac{\alpha_s}{r} + \kappa r. \label{eq:qcdpotential}
    \end{align}
    Here the first term is a Coulomb-like potential with the inclusion of a colour factor $C$ that encodes the QCD dynamics, and has to be calculated for each colour combination of the two charges. It is quite straightforward to obtain the coulomb-like term, including calculating the colour factors, by comparing it to the QED analogue.\footnote{See for instance one of the many lecture notes or textbooks on QCD, such as Modern Particle Physics by Thomson \cite{ThomsonMPP}}.

    The linear term on the other hand is far less obvious, and cannot be calculated directly from the QCD Lagrangian. Arguably the most convincing evidence comes from lattice QCD calculations, first proposed by Wilson \cite{Wilson1974} in 1974, which show that the potential between two static colour charges indeed increases linearly at large distances. This is a consequence of the non-abelian nature of QCD - the fact that gluons themselves carry colour charge and can therefore interact with each other - in combination with the structure of SU(3) and the strength of the interaction.\footnote{The strong coupling constant not only scales with the energy scale, it also depends on other parameters such as $N_c$ and $N_f$, see Eq. \ref{eq:beta0}} \tp{See above for my argument that these two are related, but the actual values of parameters are important as well.}\rs{reply: edited to add the nuances} One way to picture this is by considering a QCD dipole and comparing it to the QED analogue.

    In QED, the field lines originating from an electric charge spread out evenly in all directions, which leads to the familiar $1/r^2$ dependence of the field strength. This is a classic example of the inverse square law, which arises from the fact that the surface area of a sphere increases with $r^2$. Even in the presence of another opposite charge, the field lines still disperse in all directions, leading to the well known dipole field. In QCD however, lattice calculations show that the field lines between a quark and an antiquark tend to cluster together into a narrow tube or string, rather than spreading out. Applying the same surface area argument that leads to the inverse square law in QED, we see that the strength of the field between the quark and antiquark remains constant as they move apart - the field lines are not ``diluted'' over a larger area. This constant field strength leads directly to a linearly increasing potential. Because a linearly increasing potential corresponds to a constant force, this term can be interpreted as the energy stored in a string connecting the two colour charges, with $\kappa$ being the string tension.

  \subsection{Quark-gluon plasma}
  \label{sec:qgp}
    The fact that quarks are confined to baryons and mesons, collectively called hadrons, means that we cannot directly probe ``free''\rs{maybe use ``isolated''?} quarks and gluons. Imagining that these composite objects may be described as ``bags'' containing the quarks and gluons\footnote{It may seem like the concept of bags comes out of thin air, but it is acually a real phenomenological model called the bag model \cite{BagModel}. It can be motivated by likening the QCD vacuum to a colour-dielectric and considering the effect of anti-screening on a colour charge placed in this vacuum. This is (far) beyond the scope of this thesis, but suffice to say that invoking the bag model has some physical merit.}, it stands to reason that by increasing the density and temperature these bags may start to overlap and percolate, and at high enough densities and temperatures we may end up with a new state of matter known as the quark-gluon plasma (QGP). In this state, quarks and gluons are no longer confined to individual hadrons but may instead move freely, and hence this medium is characterised by the degrees of freedom from these partons as opposed to the hadrons we started out with. It should be noted that the word ``free'' is often used in this context, but this does not mean that the quarks and gluons are asymptotically free. Free in this context simply means unconfined, the partons still very much interact with eachother, leading to a mean free path that is much smaller than the size of the medium. This means that the QGP behaves as a fluid, and we may use thermodynamics to describe it. 

    The QGP is fascinating for a multitude of reasons. We hypothesise that a similar state of matter existed in the early universe, a few microseconds after the Big Bang, and studying it can therefore give us insights into the evolution of the early universe. Another place we might find similar extreme conditions is still far from home, in the core of neutron stars. Though it should be noted that the conditions in neutron stars are quite different from those in the early universe: the former is characterised by high baryon chemical potential and low temperature, while the latter is characterised by low baryon chemical potential and high temperature.\rs{add figure of phase diagram here?} Apart from cosmological and astrophysical considerations, the QGP offers a unique opportunity to study QCD under extreme conditions, and to probe the properties of quarks and gluons in a deconfined state. However, investigating the QGP is challenging, as one needs to create these extreme conditions in a laboratory setting, and even when succeeding in doing so, the QGP only exists for a very short time before it cools down and hadronises again.

    \subsubsection{Strange hadrons}
      Let us introduce strange hadrons and their characteristics in order to really understand the strangeness enhancement results, as well as lay the groundwork for the analysis we will present in this thesis. We will focus on hadrons that are experimentally accessible, which essentially translates charged hadrons or hadrons that decay into exclusively charged particles, emphasising the hadrons relevant for this thesis. Excited resonances and more exotic states will also largely be left out as they are only relevant for small parts of the discussion, so this is by no means a complete overview of strange hadrons. Most strange hadrons decay via the weak interaction into lighter hadrons, and this surely concerns the hadrons we will discuss here. All numbers mentioned in the next few paragraphs can readily be found on the Particle Data Group website: \url{https://pdg.lbl.gov/}. \cite{PDG2024}
      
      Starting from the lightest hadrons, we have the kaon mesons. They consist of one strange quark and one up or down quark, and remembering that one of them is an antiquark this gives four different possibilities: $K^+ (u\overline{s})$, $K^- (\overline{u}s)$, $K^0 (d\overline{s})$ and $\overline{K^0} (\overline{d}s)$. The flavour eigenstates of the neutral kaons are not the mass eigenstates - those are superpositions of the two flavour eigenstates and denoted by $K^0_S$ and $K_L^0$, after short and long lifetimes respectively. All four kaons have approximately equal mass, roughly 500 \mev. The charged kaons have a lifetime of about 12 ns, meaning that they may be considered mainly stable in the context of high energy physics experiments. This is usually determined by comparing a measure of the average distance traveled $c\tau$ to the typical size of the detector, which is on the order of a few meters. The $K^0_S$ has a $c\tau$ of few centimeters, decaying mainly into a $\pi^+\pi^-$ pair, while the $K_L^0$ has a $c\tau$ of about 15 meters and as such is not reconstructed in most experiments. It should be noted that almost all weak decaying hadrons have a lifetime of a few dozen nanoseconds or less; the $K^0_L$ is an outlier due to the decay kinematics (much like the neutron). The charged kaons are measured directly in the detector, while the $K^0_S$ is reconstructed via its decay products. 

      Next in line is the \Lambda\ baryon. Often called the ``heavier cousin'' of the proton it has a mass of 1116 \mev, compared to the 938 \mev\ proton. Like the kaons, the \Lambda\ baryon also has one strange quark but combines it with an up \textit{and} a down quark, resulting in net zero electric charge. They have two main decay modes, with the dominant one being by far the most experimentally convenient: $\Lambda (\overline{\Lambda}) \rightarrow p (\overline{p}) + \pi^- (\pi^+)$. Considering that a pion has a mass of about 140 \mev, and noting that the mass difference between the \Lambda\ and the proton is about ~180 \mev, we can see that the daughter pion is likely to be quite soft which has implications for the reconstruction of the \Lambda\ baryon. The $c\tau$ is about 8 cm, allowing us to reconstruct the \Lambda\ via its decay products, similar to the $K^0_S$. An honourable mention goes to the $\Sigma$ baryons (~1200 \mev): they also have a single strange quark combined with two light quarks, but different isospin. The charged $\Sigma$ baryons ($uus$ and $dds$) have are difficult to reconstruct as they always have a neutral hadron in their final decay products. The $\Sigma^0$ has identical quark content to the \Lambda\ and hence decays electromagnetically into a \Lambda\ and a photon, with a branching ratio of essentially 100\% and an imperceptible $c\tau$. As such, the $\Sigma^0$ contributes to the \Lambda\ yield via a its decay, called feed-down. 

      Now we will consider the \Xi\ and \Omega\ baryons. Though they have different strangeness content they are sometimes classified together under the nomer of ``cascades'', which refers to the fact that they are both experimentally reconstructed via a similar cascade of decays. The charged \Xi\ baryon has two strange quarks and one down quark, while its neutral sibling as two strange quarks and one up quark. The lack of other decay modes is explained by insufficient kinematic phase space: the sum of the masses of a $\Sigma$ baryon and a pion exceeds 1322 \mev, as is the case for a proton and a kaon. The \Omega\ baryon, consisting of three strange quarks, is a slightly different case. With a mass of 1672 \mev\ it is significantly heavier and as such leaves more kinematic room for decay modes.   \subsection{Correlations in pp collisions}

    The study of pp collisions is a crucial component of understanding heavy-ion physics, but is also interesting in its own right. In particular, most observables we use to study QCD processes unrelated to the QGP would also be affected by the presence of such a medium, take for instance the yields presented in Figure \ref{fig:strangeness_enhancement}. The hadronisation process is a typical and topical example of one such process: in heavy-ion physics it is often assumed that hadronisation is not affected by the presence of the QGP - it only happens after the QGP has cooled down - but hadron yields and correlation measurements are very much affected by the QGP. Hence, when researching hadronisation, we may want to isolate the process as best as possible by studying it in pp collisions. 

    One way of examining hadronisation is by looking at correlations between hadrons. While there are different approaches in describing hadronisation, some of which we will discuss later, almost all of them obey local conservation of quantum numbers. This is something we can probe with correlation measurements, as local conservation laws should translate to correlations in phase space between the resulting hadrons. A typical category of correlations is two-particle azimuthal correlations, where we look at the distribution of the difference in azimuthal angle \dphi\ between two particles. For hadrons that are produced in the the same hadronisation process, conserving quantum numbers, we would expect a correlation in their relative azimuthal angle.

Light enough to be produced in hadronisation processes, but heavy enough to be supressed in comparison to up and down quarks. Placed somewhere in between the light quarks and heavy quarks, they are somewhat of an edge-case, and this is often where models and theories struggle.

  The Lund String model \cite{LundModel} is based on the idea that the QCD potential between two colour charges increases linearly with distance (see Eq. \ref{eq:qcdpotential}), and as such can be described by spanning a string with tension $\kappa$ between the two charges. The value of $\kappa$ is approximately 0.2 GeV$^2$, which was already established by Eichten et al. \cite{StringTensionQuarkonium} before the conception of the Lund model %\tp{This is not fully clear. In a very simplified picture, when a quark and an antiquark move apart from each other, e.g. after a high-energy collision, they transform their kinetic energy into potential energy stored in the string. As the distance between the quarks increases, the length of the string and therefore the potential energy stored in it also increases. At some point the potential energy becomes large enough to create a new quark-antiquark pair from the vacuum, conserving all quantum numbers, which breaks the string into two smaller strings with less total length than before. This process may happen multiple times, until the remaining string pieces are too short to create new quark-antiquark pairs, at which point the quark-antiquark pairs that form string pieces are bound into a hadron.

Using the WKB approximation to write $P \propto \exp(-2S)$, where $S$ is the action, gives us a handle on the probability of this happening. \cite{StringModelSlides} With $S = \pi\sqrt{m^2 + \pt^2}/2\kappa$ this becomes:

  \end{align}

Filling reasonable approximations of these masses into Eq. \ref{eq:tunnelprob} gives a suppression factor of approximately 0.3 for strange quarks compared to up and down quarks. Taking a reasonable approximation for an up/down diquark mass as slightly less than twice the constituent quark mass of up/down quarks, we find a suppression factor of approximately 0.1 compared to single up/down quarks.

  \subsection{Extensions of the string model}
    The simple picture of a quark-antiquark pair moving apart is a rather good description in case of photon or W/Z boson decays, where the initial state is a colour singlet and the hadronisation process is constrained to the accompanying jet. Proton-proton collisions however pose two major complications to this picture: first of all, we are colliding composite objects consisting of strongly interacting partons, which means that after the initial hard scattering, the beam remnants are colour charged and hence strings will connect to them. Secondly, it is possible for multiple parton-parton interactions (MPI) to occur in a single pp collision, which are not only fundamentally correlated as the initiator partons come from the same colour-singlet (the protons), but the resulting hadronisation processes can also overlap in phase space. This means that the naive picture of considering MPIs independently is unphysical. 

    This leads to the concept of colour reconnection (CR), where the different MPI systems may combine based on their \pt\ and a tuneable parameter. In the ``default'' CR scheme, that is the scheme used in PYTHIA 8 with the Monash tune \cite{MonashTune}, the partons of the different MPI systems are reconnected in a way that minimizes the total string length. According to the PYTHIA manual \cite{PythiaManual}, this scheme can be viewed as a slightly more sophisticated version of the one introduced in \cite{firstCR}. Historically, this CR scheme was introduced to improve the description of the observed increase of average \pt\ as a function of charged particle multiplicity - if each MPI system hadronised independently, the average \pt\ would be independent of multiplicity. \cite{UA1} 

    From the experimental results discussed in Section \ref{sec:experimentalQCD}, it is clear that the default PYTHIA 8 model with Monash tune is not able to correctly describe hadronisation, with the most grating discrepancies in the baryon and strangeness sectors. Two extensions are especially relevant in this context: Beyond Leading Colour \cite{Junctions} adds additional topologies for the colour connections between partons, allowing for more complicated string topologies such as junctions. The other extension is the Rope Hadronisation model \cite{Ropes}, which allows for overlapping strings to combine into so-called ropes with a higher effective string tension, leading to an enhanced production of strange quarks and diquarks according to Eq. \ref{eq:tunnelprob}. We also briefly consider the novel Closepacking extension \cite{Closepacking}, generalising and extending the Ropes model.

With junctions, one can also produce $\Xi$\ baryons by combining two single strange quarks with a light quark. These quarks may come from parton showering or string breaking. 

      As can also be seen from Figure \ref{fig:xijunction}, in the traditional diquark picture the $\Xi$\ baryon is always connected to an anti-baryon with at least one strange quark, as they must share this strange $s\overline{s}$ pair from the diquark. In case of a double strange diquark this effect is even stronger, here a $\Xi$\ baryon will always be balanced by a $\overline{\Xi}$ or even $\overline{\Omega}$ baryon. With junctions however, the balancing anti-baryon only has one \qqbar\ pair in common with the $\Xi$\ baryon, meaning that the strangeness content can be balanced by mesons (kaons) as well as baryons. As such, strange baryon correlation measurements provide a way to experimentally probe the presence and impact of junction topologies in hadronisation.

    \subsubsection{Colour ropes}
      In situations where many strings are present in the same phase-space, one may expect these strings to interact. This idea was already proposed in 1984 by \cite{earlyRopes}, initially in the context of heavy-ion collisions. However, considering again the possibility of multiple parton interactions in pp collisions, we may expect this scenario to be applicable here as well. Generally speaking, interactions between strings can be grouped into two categories: string fusion and string shoving. String fusion refers to the idea that multiple overlapping strings may combine into a single ``rope'' with a higher effective string tension, while string shoving refers to the idea that overlapping strings may repel each other, leading to collective flow-like effects. \cite{Ropes} We will focus on the former in this thesis, and consider its effect on hadronisation. From Equation \ref{eq:tunnelprob} one can see that a higher effective string tension $\kappa$ leads to an enhanced production of heavier strange quarks and/or diquarks. This leads to an enhanced production of strange hadrons in events with higher multiplicity as these are more likely to have overlapping strings. It is not clear whether this mechanism would also affect correlations between strange hadrons. In principle we do not add a mechanism that would directly impact correlations as is the case for junctions, but as correlations are sensitive to the underlying production mechanism and ropes do fundamentally change the production mechanism, it is not unreasonable to expect some effect on correlations as well.

    \subsubsection{Closepacking}
      % The Closepacking \cite{Closepacking} model attempts to generalise and extend this idea to momentum space, treating the interactions between strings as an ``effective background'' for each string. It also adds a destructive interference   effect which is expected to reduce the diquark production rate.
      
      One of the difficulties of colour ropes is that they require spacetime modeling of the string breaks, as opposed to the general string model fragmentation which is purely in momentum space. The Closepacking model generalises the rope hadronisation picture to momentum space, describing the interactions between strings as an effective background for each string. The model also adds a destructive interference effect to the popcorn mechanism of diquark production, which essentially allows a diquark to form via consecutive \qqbar\ production. Crucially, the first \qqbar\ pair doesn't break the string but rather causes a differently coloured field (string) to be formed between the newly produced pair. See for example Figure \ref{fig:popcorninterference}, where a blue \qqbar\ pair is produced on a red string, leading to a net antigreen colour flux between blue pair. Another green \qqbar\ pair may then break the string, leading to a baryon - antibaryon pair. The novel idea of interference comes into play when there happens to be a string with blue colour flux nearby that may interfere destructively in the intermediate state, leading to meson production instead of baryon production.
