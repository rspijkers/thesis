  The bigger problem lies in the weighting of the datapoints, which happens on account of the efficiency and mixed events corrections. Let's consider the latter correction. Variations in the cascade sample due to a different topological selection will inevitably lead to a different mixed events correction. This difference should be small, as single particle effects are not expected to have a significant impact on two-particle effects, but small differences will still lead to different weights for the datapoints in the correlation analysis. These small differences violate the assumption of identical datasets. This is only exacerbated by the efficiency correction, which weights the datapoints by the inverse of the reconstruction efficiency. As the efficiency is directly affected by the topological selection criteria, these weights will fundamentally differ between the variations. 

  In practice this means that uncertainties on the ratio (difference) of the variations with respect to the default are correlated in an unknown way, and treating them as if they are fully correlated can lead to drastic under- and overestimations of the propagated uncertainties. Clear examples of either case can be seen in Figure \ref{fig:CorrSysVarLooseOS}, specifically the 7th and 14th bins. In the first case the ratio seems to deviate from 1 by approximately 6\% with a very small uncertainty, which happens when the two datapoints have almost identical absolute uncertainties but different values. In the second case the ratio seems to be almost exactly 1 but with a large uncertainty, here the exact opposite happens - the two datapoints have almost identical values but different uncertainties. This clearly cannot happen when the datasets are identical (either unweighted or both weighted in the exact same manner), as under this assumption two datapoints with identical values would have identical uncertainties. 

  There is unfortunately not much we can do about this, since the efficiency correction encodes the effect of the topological selection criteria which is exactly what we're trying to probe. We have considered removing the mixed events correction under the assumption that it should remain the same between the variations - the entire correction would then cancel in the ratio between the variation anyway. Indeed this does improve the behaviour of the uncertainties, but it does not remove the problem entirely. On top of this, for the OS - SS correlations we estimate the systematic uncertainty from the absolute difference between the variation and the default, so the mixed events corrections do not cancel. As the correction is applied by increasing the weight of the datapoints away from \dy\ and \dphi\ equal to zero, removing the correction would lead to an underestimation of the systematic uncertainty. \rs{maybe mention here what could be done in the future?}

  When considering the ratio (difference) of the variations, we observe no clear behaviour as a function of \dphi. Based on this we decide to calculate the systematic uncertainty as a constant fit to the ratio (difference). Even this can be done in multiple ways, and to investigate which would be the most robust we look at three different methods:
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item The unweighted arithmetic mean of the ratio (difference) across \dphi.
    \item The weighted arithmetic mean of the ratio (difference) across \dphi.
    \item The ratio of sums (difference of averages). Here we sum (average) first across \dphi\ before calculating the ratio (difference). 
  \end{enumerate}

  In all three methods we assume no bin-to-bin correlation of the uncertainties. The results of the different methods are shown in Figure \ref{fig:SystematicAvgTightOS} for the tight variation of the OS correlation. We have chosen this specific case to highlight the difference between the methods, but the considerations hold for all cases. It is immediately clear that the weighted average places undue importance on datapoints with small uncertainties, as these uncertainties are likely underestimated as discussed above. In this specific example this will lead to an overestimation of the systematic uncertainty. Both the unweighed average and the ratio of sums give a more reasonable estimate of the systematic uncertainty, and the agreement between the two is a sign that both methods are robust against any outliers. In theory we expect the ratio of sums to be slightly more robust against outliers as they are ``integrated out'' before calculating the ratio, so we decide to use this method to determine the average. Indeed, even momentarily disregarding the uncertainties on the datapoints, the spread of the ratio across \dphi\ is rather large. This is a sign that we suffer from a limited amount of statistics in determining the systematic uncertainty. For this reason we decide to estimate the systematic uncertainty from the topological variations with the systematic uncertainty obtained for the spectra analysis. This is motivated by the fact that the OS and SS correlations are defined as a trigger-normalised pair yield, which in essence comes down to an associate yield, a similar quantity to the single particle yield that we calculate for the spectra. \rs{$<$-- formulate this better?} Where we could straightforwardly take the \pt\ dependent systematic uncertainty for the \pt\ spectrum, the \pt\ dependence of the correlation results is not clear. As the \Xi\ baryon yield is higher for lower \pt\, we should definitely take this into account when determining the systematic uncertainty for the correlation analysis. For this reason we take a flat 1.5\% relative systematic uncertainty corresponding to the maximum deviation observed in Figure \ref{fig:SpectraRatioSys} for \pt\ between 1 and 2 GeV/$c$. 

  Applying this 1.5\% to the OS - SS correlation would only work under the assumption that it too is a quantity equivalent to a single particle yield, which is not so clear. The systematic uncertainties for the OS and SS are likely to be correlated in a way that leads to a significant reduction of the systematic uncertainty for the OS - SS, so we propagate the magnitudes of the systematic uncertainties for the OS and SS to the OS - SS assuming they are fully correlated. We do this since the associate \Xi\ baryons are in either case an average over both signs. However, this method unsurprisingly leads to a negligible systematic uncertainty on the awayside, where the OS and SS correlations take similar values. We suspect that this is an underestimation of the systematic uncertainty, as there will be sources of the systematic uncertainty that are not fully correlated between the OS and SS. To take this into account, we take the systematic uncertainty obtained from the correlation analysis as the lower limit for the systematic uncertainty of the OS - SS correlation, which is defined as the average of the absolute values of the systematic uncertainties obtained from the tight and loose variations. 

  \section{Other sources of sysetmatic uncertainties}

  In much the same way as the spectra analysis, we want to take the systematic uncertainty from the material budget into account. As a \pt\ dependent systematic uncertainty is not applicable to the correlation analysis, we take a flat 3\% uncertainty across all \dphi\ bins. At first glance this may seem as an overestimation based on Figure \ref{fig:MaterialBudget}, but as most \Xi\ baryons are reconstructed at low \pt\ we choose to have this region guide our estimate of the systematic uncertainty. In the future one may want to do a more detailed study of the effect of the material budget uncertainty on the correlation results by varying the detector material budget in the MC simulations that are used to compute the \Xi\ baryon reconstruction efficiency. 

  We have also considered the sideband definition as a potential source of systematic uncertainty, as these are used to estimate the contribution of the combinatorial background to the OS and OS - SS correlations. By choosing an alternate sideband definition $[\mu + 4\sigma, \mu + 8\sigma]$ (and the corresponding window chosen symmetrically on the other side of the peak) and comparing to the default definition $[\mu + 4\sigma, \mu + 10\sigma]$ we see a small but significant difference in the correlation result. This is taken as a flat uncertainty across all \dphi\ bins for the OS and SS correlations, but not for the OS - SS correlation as the difference was not significant in this case. 

  Finally, we have investigated the non-closure of the MC closure test. As the closure test is consistent within 1 roger-barlow standard deviation, we do not consider this as a source of systematic uncertainty.
